Understanding German Degree Pathways

Germany’s education system is slightly different when compared to other countries. Let’s explore how qualifications vary across each degree stream:

Bachelor’s Degree (3 to 4 Years)

Part of undergraduate studies; an academic degree is not required to begin. Teaches the core basics of a subject matter. Bachelor of Science (B.Sc.) is awarded in science fields and Bachelor of Arts (B.A.) in humanities. It permits lower-level entry into the job market, though students can seamlessly transition into a master's degree.

Master’s Degree (1 to 2 Years)

Refers to obtaining a post-graduate university qualification. Comprises learning and expanding advanced knowledge while specialising in one to two fields of interest (e.g., Master of Science (M.Sc.) or Master of Engineering (M.Eng.)).

State Examinations (Staatsexamen)

Required for specific academic subjects such as becoming a lawyer, doctor, or pharmacist. These subjects sit outside the normal bachelor/master track. Students undergo rigorous professional training before taking a second state exam to carry their professional title under strict federal regulations.

Doctorate (PhD - 2+ Years)

For students pursuing academic research careers after their Master’s degree. Consists of researching, writing, and publishing a dissertation. Admission is dependent on independently finding a university professor willing to formally accept you as a PhD student.

Dual Vocational Degree (3 to 5 Years)

A special program integrating higher education studies with paid on-the-job training in a company (common in business and engineering). Requires academic entrance qualifications. Students must first apply directly to an approved company for practical training responsibilities before enrolling at the cooperating university.

Fees, Scholarships, and Cost of Living

Cost of Tuition and Semester Fees

As a general rule, students do not pay tuition fees for standard Bachelor's or Master's degrees at state higher education institutions. However, unique rules apply based on location and specific university selection:

•

Public Universities: €0 Tuition (Free for regular Bachelor/Master courses)

•

Baden-Württemberg State: €1,500 per semester (For non-EU citizens entering since winter 2017/18; completely exempt for PhD students)

•

Private Higher Institutions: €7,000 to €36,500 per academic year (Depending on the university and program type)

•

Semester Contribution: €100 to €350 per semester (Paid to student services and the AstA student union)

•

Semester Transit Ticket: Included in many institutional contributions, costing between €25 to €200 for regional public transport travel passes

Scholarship Programs for International Students

Direct institutional funding inside Germany can be highly competitive due to incredibly narrow selection criteria. However, major structural funding models remain available to support qualified individuals:

DAAD & Foundations: The German Academic Exchange Service (DAAD) coordinates significant scholarship frameworks for undergraduate, postgraduate, and research tracks. Political, corporate, and religious organizations also distribute notable financial grants.

Erasmus+ Framework: Open to students from the 27 EU states as well as associated countries like Iceland, Turkey, Macedonia, Norway, and Liechtenstein. It offers tuition exemptions and monthly grants structured on European Commission guidelines for 3-to-12-month placements.

Admission Process

University Entrance Qualifications

Admissions pathways rely completely on your country of origin and whether you hold an EU or non-EU high school qualification certificate. Evaluation authorities verify options such as your High School Diploma, Matura, A-Levels, or Bachillerato.

Non-EU Citizens: You must verify your credentials using the DAAD Admissions Database or the German Anabin Database to see if your certificate qualifies for general entry, subject-specific entry, or requires supplementary home study. (As Anabin is often completely in German, our counselors can navigate this for you).

The Studienkolleg Track: If your secondary school certificate isn't deemed equivalent, you must complete a preparatory college entry exam, acquire advanced German, and pass a subject-relevant assessment test (Feststellungsprüfung) to gain admission. Taking the TestAS aptitude exam also drastically builds your candidate profiling score.

EU & EEA Citizens: Students from European countries (and Switzerland, Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein) skip general visa requirements. If your degree matches the German Abitur framework, you receive equal placement metrics. Stays over 3 months simply require local address registration at the university town town hall.

Required Document Checklist for Submission:

✓

Certified copies of language proficiency certificates

✓

Officially certified copy of university/college entrance qualifications

✓

Officially certified copies of previous university certificates (if available)

Note: Documents must be officially certified German translations, though some universities accept certified English or French paperwork.

FAQs

Can I study in Germany even if I don’t know the language?

Yes, Germany offers thousands of international courses taught entirely in English, particularly at the postgraduate level. However, basic conversational German is highly recommended to manage daily administrative tasks, secure part-time employment, and ease social integration.

What does University Entrance Qualification mean?

Known as the Hochschulzugangsberechtigung (HZB), this is a formal evaluation showing whether your school leaving certificate (such as a high school diploma or A-levels) satisfies prerequisites to enter a degree course at a German higher education institution.

What if my University Entrance Qualification Certificate does not qualify me to enter a university in Germany?

You will be required to attend a one-year preparatory foundation college (Studienkolleg). After passing the initial entrance test, you will take an assessment test (Feststellungsprüfung) in subjects relevant to your major to gain standard university qualification eligibility.

How do I prove that I have sufficient funds to study in Germany?

You must demonstrate stable financial capacity to cover living costs. Non-EU applicants typically fulfill this requirement by setting up a federally regulated Blocked Account (Sperrkonto) with minimum required living deposits, or by presenting an official Letter of Commitment from a local resident.

What is the best DAAD Scholarship I can apply for studying in Germany?

The DAAD organizes distinct scholarship structures tailored directly for graduates completing full Master's courses, as well as separate specialized Research Grants for PhD tracks. Our Expert Counselors can review your academic history to identify the ideal program.

Can I work while studying in Germany?

Yes, international students have statutory part-time employment allowances. Non-EU students are legally permitted to work up to 140 full days or 280 half days per calendar year. This allows you to earn supplemental income while getting directly involved with local workplace culture.

Which exams are required for studying in Germany?

This depends on your course track and entry status. Requirements may include language proficiency exams (such as TestDaF/DSH for German streams or TOEFL/IELTS for English streams), the Studienkolleg entrance examination, or the TestAS academic aptitude test.

Is it necessary to learn the German language to study a course in Germany?

If your chosen degree is formally classified as an English-medium program, German is not mandatory for institutional admission. However, acquiring basic language skills is strongly recommended to easily navigate immigration offices, sign rental contracts, and access broader job networks.
